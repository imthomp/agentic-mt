\section*{Where the theory stands}

The central joint-necessity claim has not been confirmed or falsified --- every direct test
has hit a model confound first. We do have real evidence against the simplest instantiations
of two supporting mechanisms (QE-in-the-loop, source-side triggering), and a new finding, from one model across five language points (BLOOMZ, Phase 6), that
raw pretraining exposure to a language can be a hard gate on generation --- more a threshold
effect than a smooth curve --- separate from anything the agentic scaffolding does.

\section*{Findings, by implication}

\noindent\footnotesize\textit{None of the thresholds/p-values below correct for multiple
comparisons across the six phases --- read each as suggestive on its own terms, not as part of a
jointly-controlled family.}\normalsize

\subsection*{Challenges to specific proposed mechanisms}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{QE-in-the-loop (Sec.~5.3) is not viable as specified for most of the
    5 low-resource pairs tested (ha/km/ps/xh/zu).} An en-de benchmark of $\approx\PhaseOneEndeBenchmarkSpearman$ sets the bar these
    pairs miss. This confirms, rather than merely illustrates, the risk the proposal itself
    flags in Sec.~5.3 (``reference-free QE models... must be adapted for language pairs with
    limited QE training data'') --- the mechanism's foundation is measurably weaker exactly
    where the theory says it should matter most. To trust more: extend beyond the five pairs
    with real human judgments; current coverage is a hard ceiling on what we can check.
  \item \textbf{Source-side triggering (Sec.~5.1) shows no signal from three of the five
    features that section names} (OOV rate, sentence length, idiomatic density) \textbf{on
    these same 5 pairs,} against a chance baseline of $\PhaseTwoAucChance$. The pooled OOV
    correlation evaporates to $\PhaseTwoOovPerlangLo$--$\PhaseTwoOovPerlangHi$ once controlled
    for language. To trust more: a real domain classifier and a frequency-list OOV measure
    (the two named features not yet tested) before calling triggering a dead end rather than
    an implementation gap.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{The central joint-necessity claim: confounded, not resolved}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Phase 3}: Aya-101 is not conversation-tuned, and its CoT chain produced
    byte-identical draft\slash refine\slash proofread output in
    \PhaseThreeCotVerbatimRepeatEnde--\PhaseThreeCottoolVerbatimRepeatEnha{} of
    \PhaseThreeVerbatimRepeatDenominator{} sentences (Fig.~1) --- this degeneracy confounds any
    reasoning-quality comparison before we even look at scores. Given that: the raw numbers
    (tool-alone wins both pairs; CoT scores lowest; gap differs by resource level,
    $p=\PhaseThreeGapTestPLo$--$\PhaseThreeGapTestPHi$) describe what happened, but should be read
    as ``a broken CoT chain scored worse,'' not as evidence about reasoning per se. Worth
    flagging even with that discount: CoT+Tool scored below Tool-alone on \emph{both} pairs ---
    the joint-necessity claim predicts the combination should beat either pillar alone, and the
    raw direction here runs the other way. The degeneracy is a sufficient explanation, but we
    haven't shown it's the \emph{only} one; this isn't evidence for the theory even discounted.
  \item \textbf{Phase 4}: matches Feng et al.'s actual TEaR pattern, not the early-peak
    hypothesis we set out to test (that pattern belongs to a different paper). Secondary
    finding: condition A froze only \PhaseFourCondAFrozenByIterFour/\PhaseFourNTotal{} sentences
    by iteration 4 vs.\ condition B's \PhaseFourCondBFrozenByIterThree/\PhaseFourNTotal{} by
    iteration 3, and A's trajectory is smoother --- span-level feedback may matter more than
    feedback source. To trust more: rerun condition B with XCOMET (span-level) to isolate
    granularity from source. \emph{Relevance to the central claim:} this phase tests refinement
    quality, not joint necessity directly --- it neither confirms nor challenges the central
    claim, though the granularity finding is worth folding into whichever joint-necessity test
    runs next. \emph{Gap against the proposal:} Sec.~5.3 predicts the undirected-vs-QE-directed
    refinement gap ``should be largest in low-resource settings''; this phase tested exactly
    those two conditions but only on en-de, so it cannot speak to that specific prediction ---
    still open.
  \item \textbf{Phases 5--6}: two replacement-model candidates ruled out with direct evidence,
    not assumption (see table). \textbf{Net: the joint-necessity question is still untested on
    a model without a disqualifying confound.}
  \item \textbf{New: best-of-$\PhaseThreeBestofnN$ Level-1 baseline, filling Sec.~7's (b)-vs-(c)
    gap.} Sec.~7 specifies comparing inference-time-scaled Level 1 (best-of-$N$/MBR) against
    full agentic Level 2 as ``particularly important''; none of Phase 3's four conditions did
    this. Ran it for en-ha: best-of-$\PhaseThreeBestofnN$ temperature sampling, no tools, no
    reasoning, scored $\PhaseThreeBestofnEnhaCometkiwi$ COMET-KIWI --- higher than \emph{all
    four} architected conditions, including tool-alone ($0.602$). Taken at face value this is
    an uncomfortable answer to Sec.~7's question: compute alone beat architecture. \textbf{But
    it can't be taken at face value:} selection here used COMET-KIWI as judge, and Phase 1 measured COMET-KIWI's correlation with real human judgment for Hausa specifically at just
    $\PhaseOneHaSpearman$ --- the weakest of all five pairs. This may be rewarding samples that
    exploit COMET-KIWI's blind spots rather than ones that are genuinely better. Needs
    rerunning scored by something Phase 1 didn't already flag as unreliable for this pair
    before treating the result as settled --- but it's the check Sec.~7 asks for, and as
    measured it did not favor the architecture.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Tangential but new: a pretraining-exposure floor}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Phase 6}: below some exposure level BLOOMZ doesn't attempt the target language
    at all --- it defaults to echoing the English source (comprehension intact, generation
    not) --- rather than degrading gracefully. Adding Yoruba ($\sim$90M ROOTS bytes, between
    Swahili's 236M and Xhosa/Zulu's 14.3M/8.5M) as the planned intermediate point produced
    0\% clean output, matching Xhosa/Zulu rather than sitting between them and Swahili's 50\%
    --- so this looks like a threshold somewhere between 90M and 236M bytes, not the smooth
    dose-response curve the four-point version suggested. To trust more: still only 5 points
    (this model only); a language between 90M and 236M bytes would tell us whether this is a
    sharp cutoff or just a steep slope. \emph{Possible challenge to Sec.~4:} the below-floor
    outputs aren't flawed translations a tool could correct --- they're the English source
    echoed back, meaning there's no partial competence left to ground. Sec.~4 argues external
    resources carry \emph{more} marginal value as neural coverage thins; that argument
    implicitly assumes some translation attempt exists to augment. If this generalizes beyond
    BLOOMZ, the marginal-value argument may have a floor below which it inverts to zero, not
    just a low: the tool would have to generate the whole translation, not inform one.
  \item Cross-checked for Phase 3's Aya-101/mT5: German has $\PhaseSixMtFiveDeTokensB$B tokens
    in mC4 vs.\ Hausa's $\PhaseSixMtFiveHaTokensB$B ($\sim\PhaseSixMtFiveDeHaRatio\times$) --- so
    Phase 3's en-de anchor \emph{is} legitimate for that model, unlike BLOOM's zero-exposure
    German. Same question, opposite answer for the two models --- check per-model, don't assume.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{What we know regardless of how the central claim resolves}

Three results don't wait on an unconfounded joint-necessity test: QE-in-the-loop misses its
threshold on \PhaseOneNPairsFlagged/\PhaseOneNPairsTotal{} tested pairs, source-side triggering
shows no signal on those same pairs, and BLOOMZ's generation reliability is gated by pretraining
exposure (below, now five points). These are usable in the paper draft now, independent of which
option we pick next.

\subsection*{Scope against the proposal}

Two honesty notes. First, ha/km/ps/xh/zu were chosen because real WMT human judgments exist
for them, not because they're confirmed as the Matrix Lab's actual target portfolio --- worth
checking those match before leaning on these results for portfolio-specific decisions. Second,
these six phases only touch Secs.~5.1--5.3; document-level session management (Sec.~5.4),
parallel hypothesis exploration (Sec.~5.5), and interactive interpretation with clarification
(Sec.~5.6, the clinical MT program's home turf) are untested here.

\section*{Open decision}

Three ways to get an unconfounded joint-necessity result:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Keep searching} for an open model that is conversation-tuned and covers the
    target languages. Two candidates already ruled out; no known third, and preserves an
    all-open-source stack.
  \item \textbf{Split the two roles}: an API model for reasoning, a Church-fine-tuned NLLB
    model queried as the tool. Sidesteps Phase 6's exposure floor (NLLB-200 targets all five pairs by design, though
    we have not verified its per-language training scale the way Phase 6 did for BLOOM/mT5 ---
    that check should happen before committing to this option) and more faithfully tests the
    theory's actual claim
    (reasoning \emph{and} tool use, not one model doing both). Costs API spend, drops the
    fully-open constraint.
  \item \textbf{Narrow scope}: run the test on one pair BLOOMZ already generates reliably
    (en-fr, $\PhaseSixEnfrCleanRatePct\%$ clean), treat low-resource generalization as future
    work.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent\textbf{Leaning toward option 2} --- it resolves Phase 6's confound directly rather
than continuing to search for a model that clears it. Option 3 is the fallback if preserving an
open-only stack matters more than resolving the question now.

\section*{What changes in the paper draft}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Sec.~5.3 (QE-in-the-loop)}: explicit caveat --- doesn't work for
    \PhaseOneNPairsFlagged/\PhaseOneNPairsTotal{} tested pairs; reframe as open problem.
  \item \textbf{Sec.~5.1 (triggering)}: same --- cheap features show no signal; only crude
    implementations ruled out so far.
  \item \textbf{Sec.~1 (motivation)}: add the exposure-floor finding as a distinct failure mode
    from reasoning/tool insufficiency.
  \item \textbf{Core joint-necessity claim}, wherever stated: needs
    ``confounded/untested cleanly'' framing --- not silence, not overclaiming from Phase 3.
  \item \textbf{Sec.~7 (evaluation protocol)}: the (b)-vs-(c) best-of-$N$ comparison it
    specifies is now partially filled in (en-ha, one model) --- as measured, compute alone
    beat every architected condition, though the QE judge used for selection is one Phase 1
    already flagged as unreliable for this pair. Needs a trustworthy-metric rerun before this
    result can inform the draft either way.
\end{itemize}

\section*{Appendix: debugging record (compressed)}

\noindent\footnotesize\textit{Every entry below is a bug that was caught only after it produced
a confusing result. Phases 1 and 2's results have not had equivalent adversarial scrutiny applied
to them --- treat their clean appearance with that base rate in mind, not as proof nothing is
wrong.}\normalsize

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Aya-101 (Phase 3)}: T5 seq2seq, no chat template; flattened multi-turn context
    to a role-tagged transcript --- fine for single-turn conditions, degenerated for the 4-step
    CoT chain.
  \item \textbf{Refine-step scoring bug (Phase 4)}: Qwen2.5-32B's refine responses were often
    full English explanations, entirely scored as ``the translation'' --- a fake collapse from
    $\PhaseFourRefineBugFirstRunIterZeroCometkiwi{} \to \PhaseFourRefineBugFirstRunIterOneCometkiwi$
    COMET-KIWI. Fixed with an ``output only the translation'' instruction plus an extractor,
    validated against the original buggy data
    ($\PhaseFourExtractFixSuccessCount$/$\PhaseFourExtractFixTotalCount$ cleaned correctly).
  \item \textbf{BLOOMZ/InkubaLM (Phase 5)}: \PhaseFiveNBugsFixedBloomzInkubalm{} fixes --- missing
    pre-download, an outdated KV-cache path in InkubaLM's custom model class, partial
    gated-repo caching, a context-window miscalculation, role-tag framing that made both models
    echo prompt boilerplate, a cue-removal that caused empty output, a misdiagnosed
    repetition-penalty fix, a cue-induced truncation bug, and finally a genuine
    correct-vs.-echo reliability problem that wasn't further fixable. An earlier full run that
    looked like a contradiction of Phase 3 was explicitly retracted once these bugs were
    understood.
  \item \textbf{ROOTS-table correction (Phase 6)}: a secondary characterization claimed
    Xhosa/Zulu were absent from BLOOM's pretraining data. The primary source (BLOOM paper,
    Table 1) shows both present with real, small byte counts. The claim that German has
    \emph{zero} presence held up, though --- and turned out to be the more consequential
    finding.
\end{itemize}
